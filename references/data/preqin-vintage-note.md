# Preqin Vintage-Dispersion Note

A **categorical note**, not a precise data pull — by design (2026-05-30
decision). Preqin's current per-vintage quartile tables are subscription-gated;
this file captures the *shape* of private-fund return dispersion from publicly
citable analysis, so `05-benchmark-returns.md` can make one essential LP point:
**manager and vintage selection dominate private-fund outcomes**, which is why a
single headline IRR is nearly meaningless without the dispersion around it.

**As of:** 2001–2017 fund vintages (latest publicly citable study window)
**Captured:** 2026-06-06
**Source:** CAIS, citing Preqin / private-markets benchmark data (public
secondary citation)

## The dispersion pattern (private real estate, net IRR)

| Strategy | Median net IRR | Interquartile range (Q1–Q3 spread) |
|---|---|---|
| Core / core-plus | 10.0% | 8.4% — the *narrowest* dispersion |
| Value-added | 12.4% | 12.2% |
| Opportunistic | 11.0% | 13.0% — the *widest* dispersion |

*(2001–2017 vintages, net-to-LP IRR. Source: CAIS, citing private-markets
benchmark data.)*

Two LP-lens takeaways:

1. **Risk shows up as dispersion, not just lower median.** Opportunistic funds
   carry a *wider* outcome spread (13.0% IQR) than core (8.4%) despite a similar
   median — the higher-octane strategy's risk is the width of the cone, not a
   lower midpoint. An LP picking an opportunistic deal is making a manager bet
   far more than a strategy bet.
2. **A top-quartile vs bottom-quartile gap of ~8–13 points** means the same
   strategy, same vintage, produces wildly different LP outcomes depending on the
   GP. This is the empirical backing for treating an unrealized or
   selectively-presented track record skeptically (`03` → `GEN-05`, `GEN-14`):
   in a field this dispersed, a GP showing only its winners tells you almost
   nothing.

## Vintage timing

Across vintages, private-RE peer groups show significant dispersion with
multiple-basis spreads on the order of 0.6x–0.9x — i.e. *when* you commit
(the vintage year, and the entry point in the cycle it implies) materially moves
realized returns. This grounds the LP question of vintage/cycle timing rather
than treating a deal's projected IRR as cycle-independent.

## How `05` uses this

Not as a benchmark *number* (the ETF and NPI snapshots supply those) but as the
**dispersion caveat** on every private-return comparison: when a deal claims a
top-quartile-looking IRR, `05` should remind the LP that (a) top-quartile is a
narrow door, (b) the median is materially lower, and (c) the bottom quartile is
a real and common outcome. The illiquidity premium an LP demands should price
this dispersion, not just the median.
