# Asset Class Norms (LP perspective)

A factual baseline for screening passive deals. The ranges here are **screening
heuristics, not underwriting** — use them to spot when a deal's claimed return,
hold, or risk profile is far enough outside category norm to warrant deeper
questions, and to flag absent disclosures against the typical baseline.

## How to read this file

- **LP lens only.** Every field is from the passive limited-partner perspective:
  what the LP receives **net of fees**, what hold the fund commits to, what risk
  the LP carries. Nothing here covers underwriting for acquisition, asset
  management, or operator economics.
- **"Variable" is a real value.** Where current-cycle conditions make a
  category's norms genuinely unstable (post-2020 office, regulatory-sensitive
  STR, cycle-sensitive development), the cell says *variable* with a one-line
  reason. *Variable* is the file's honest answer — it tells the analyst the
  baseline is the **deal's own underwriting**, not a category default.
- **Absent disclosures flag against this baseline.** A deal that omits hold
  period, fee structure, or return expectation when the category baseline has
  one is a flag, not a neutral silence.
- **Ranges are net to LP, not gross.** Gross IRRs are not directly comparable
  across categories — fee stacks differ enough that gross-on-gross misleads.
- **Provenance is categorical.** "Industry survey norms" means the broad
  consensus across NCREIF (NPI, ODCE), Preqin private-markets data, ILPA LP
  performance reports, sector trade groups (NMHC, NAIOP, ICSC, MBA), and a
  cross-section of LP marketing materials from the major platforms. When the
  cycle shifts faster than this file, the file lags — re-baseline against
  recent realized deal flow.

## Multifamily — value-add

Stabilized acquisitions with a renovation / reposition thesis (unit interiors,
amenities, operations). The dominant retail-LP syndication category.

- **Hold.** 5–7 years; refi-at-stabilization business plans sometimes extend.
- **Returns.** Target net IRR 14–18%, equity multiple 1.5–2.0x. Realized
  performance across vintages tends to cluster 12–15% net IRR blended (ILPA /
  NCREIF realized data), with wider dispersion than the marketing range
  suggests. Cash-on-cash 5–8% after stabilization.
- **Key LP risks.** Rent-growth assumption (slowing-cycle deals are exposed),
  **exit cap rate** (the single biggest swing in most pro formas), and refi
  risk (if the plan depends on a refi at lower rates).
- **Essential disclosures.** Rent roll, T-12 actuals (not just trailing-3 or
  trailing-6), pro forma exit cap with sensitivity, debt structure and maturity,
  refi assumptions, GP track record on **prior multifamily exits** (not just
  acquisitions).

## Multifamily — core / core-plus

Stabilized, high-occupancy assets with light or no value-add. Lower risk,
lower return.

- **Hold.** 7–10 years; cash-flow-driven.
- **Returns.** Net IRR 8–12%. Cash flow dominates over exit appreciation.
- **Key LP risks.** Cap-rate compression / decompression; interest-rate
  sensitivity on cash flow when fixed-rate debt rolls.
- **Essential disclosures.** Occupancy history (5+ years), T-12 NOI, full debt
  structure (rate, term, amortization, prepayment), exit cap assumption.

## Industrial

Logistics, warehouse, last-mile, light industrial. Benefited from a structural
e-commerce tailwind through the 2010s and 2020s.

- **Hold.** 5–10 years.
- **Returns.** Value-add 10–15% net IRR; stabilized core 7–10%.
- **Key LP risks.** Tenant credit (often single-tenant or few-tenant assets),
  lease expirations vs business-plan exit, market vacancy in the submarket
  (not just metro-wide), replacement cost vs the deal's basis.
- **Essential disclosures.** Tenant credit profile, lease term and rent steps,
  submarket vacancy and absorption, replacement cost benchmark.

## Retail — necessity (grocery-anchored / service-oriented)

Recession-resistant retail with daily-needs anchors and high traffic.

- **Hold.** 7–10 years.
- **Returns.** Net IRR 8–12%.
- **Key LP risks.** Anchor lease term and anchor credit (loss of an anchor can
  cascade through cotenancy clauses), inline tenant rollover, parking ratio in
  markets where it matters.
- **Essential disclosures.** Anchor lease term and rent, anchor credit rating
  or sales-PSF, cotenancy clauses, inline tenant roll schedule.

## Retail — experiential / large-format / mall

*Variable.* The post-2020 omnichannel shift hit traditional retail unevenly:
malls and big-box are uniformly stressed, while curated experiential retail
(entertainment, lifestyle centers in high-income trade areas) is highly
deal-specific. **Treat any range as a deal-by-deal underwriting, not a
category baseline.**

- **Key LP risks.** Tenant rent-to-sales ratios (a healthy retailer at 8–10%;
  a stressed retailer at 15%+ is a default candidate), lease term remaining,
  whether replacement tenants are realistically available at the assumed rent.
- **Essential disclosures.** Tenant-by-tenant rent and sales, lease expirations,
  cotenancy exposure, any tenant credit warnings (Moody's / S&P watch lists).

## Office

*Variable.* The post-2020 occupancy reset is still working through (2025–26).
Class-A trophy in resilient markets (Sun Belt, secondary growth metros) is
holding up; class-B and class-C, especially in commute-heavy CBDs, is
dislocated. Pricing creates both opportunity and value-trap. **A claimed return
for "office" with no class / market / WFH-mix disclosure is unscreenable.**

- **Key LP risks.** **Physical** occupancy (not just leased — leased space can
  sit dark), sublease overhang in the submarket, debt maturity timing vs
  business plan, market reabsorption pace.
- **Essential disclosures.** Class (A trophy vs A vs B vs C), submarket
  reabsorption rate, physical occupancy, sublease overhang, lease expiration
  schedule, debt maturity, capex required to compete (move-in-ready vs
  vanilla shell).

## Mixed-Use

Composite of residential / retail / office / parking / hotel / other. **Risk
is dominated by the weakest or most-uncertain component, not the mix average.**

- **Hold.** Component-weighted.
- **Returns.** *Variable* — defer to the deepest underwritten component;
  if one component is "variable" (office, experiential retail), the deal's
  return is variable.
- **Essential disclosures.** Revenue breakdown by component, sensitivity
  analysis if a softening component drops by a stated %, capex allocation
  by component.

## Single Family Rental (SFR)

Institutionalized residential rental, often via aggregators. A maturing
asset class (2010s–2020s growth) with shorter institutional track record than
multifamily.

- **Hold.** 5–10 years; cash-flow-driven.
- **Returns.** Net IRR 8–14%.
- **Key LP risks.** Market-level SFR rent and tenant demand, property age and
  capex schedule (SFR has more dispersed capex than multifamily), eviction
  process exposure by jurisdiction, scale economics (a 50-home portfolio is
  not the same business as a 5,000-home portfolio).
- **Essential disclosures.** Market-level rent and absorption, average property
  age and capex reserve, geographic concentration, operator scale and
  property-management approach.

## Short-Term Rental (STR)

Per-property or fund. Regulatory environment and operational quality dominate
returns by a wide margin.

- **Hold.** 3–7 years.
- **Returns.** *Variable* — a strong STR market with stable regulation can
  return 15%+ net; the same property under restrictive municipal regulation
  can return low single digits or be forced into long-term-rental conversion.
- **Key LP risks.** Local regulatory regime **and its trajectory** (the
  regulatory cycle is the dominant risk), operator track record on ADR /
  occupancy, dependency on a single platform's ranking algorithm, fixed-cost
  coverage in low season.
- **Essential disclosures.** Local STR regulation today and any pending
  ordinances, operator's realized (not projected) ADR / occupancy on
  comparable properties, fixed-cost breakeven occupancy.

## Development (ground-up)

Entitlement, construction, lease-up, exit. High return, high risk, cycle-timing
exposure.

- **Hold.** 3–5 years.
- **Returns.** Target net IRR 18–22%. Realized returns often below target on
  construction-cost overruns, financing-cost spikes, or lease-up misses.
- **Key LP risks.** Entitlement status (entitled-but-not-permitted is a
  different deal than shovel-ready), construction-cost contingency, debt
  structure (construction-to-perm vs separate takeout), market absorption,
  GP's specific development experience (development experience is not
  fungible with acquisition experience).
- **Essential disclosures.** Entitlement and permit status, hard-cost budget
  with contingency %, debt structure including takeout assumptions, market
  absorption assumptions with comp evidence, GP's last three development
  exits with realized timing and budget vs underwritten.

## Hard Money / Bridge Loan (fund or direct)

Senior secured short-duration debt against real estate, typically for fix-and-
flip or quick-acquisition borrowers.

- **Hold.** 6–18 months per loan; fund-level commitment 3–5 years.
- **Returns.** Net IRR to LP 8–10%; gross 11–13% before fees is common.
- **Risk position.** Senior secured. Typical LTV 65–75% on **ARV** (after-repair
  value, not as-is) — note the distinction; an ARV-based 70% LTV can be 100%+
  on current value.
- **Key LP risks.** Average portfolio LTV on **as-is** value (not just ARV),
  default and foreclosure process competence (workouts are a craft skill),
  geographic concentration, fund-level credit losses through prior cycles
  (especially 2020 and 2022–23 rate spikes).
- **Essential disclosures.** Average loan LTV (as-is and ARV), default rate
  history, foreclosure process and recovery rate, geographic mix, sponsor
  history through a downturn.

## Private Credit (fund)

Senior secured private corporate or real-estate lending. Broader than just
real estate.

- **Hold.** 3–7 years (committed capital, with distribution schedule).
- **Returns.** Net IRR 8–12%.
- **Risk position.** Senior secured corporate loans; recovery rates historically
  ~60–80% on defaults, but with wide dispersion by vintage and credit quality.
- **Key LP risks.** Portfolio diversification (number of loans, sector
  concentration), fund-level leverage (BDC-style leverage amplifies both
  returns and losses), seniority position in the capital stack, vintage
  exposure to credit cycles.
- **Essential disclosures.** Loan count and average loan size, sector mix,
  fund leverage ratio, seniority position, historical default rate and
  recovery rate (preferably through-cycle).

## Other

If the deal doesn't fit a named category, that mismatch is itself a flag.
Apply this file's discipline to the closest analog (e.g. "this is structured
like a hard-money fund but secured by mobile-home parks") and ask the GP why
the analog doesn't apply.

## Provenance

- **NCREIF.** NPI (National Property Index) and ODCE (Open-end Diversified Core
  Equity Fund Index) for institutional realized real-estate returns.
- **Preqin.** Cross-category private-markets performance data.
- **ILPA.** LP-side reporting standards and aggregated LP performance.
- **Sector trade groups.** NMHC (multifamily), NAIOP (industrial / office /
  commercial), ICSC (retail), MBA (debt).
- **LP marketing.** Cross-section of recent offerings from EquityMultiple,
  CrowdStreet, RealtyMogul, direct GP emails.
